Discover

The First 5 Seconds

We all have a spot by the door. A chair, a counter, a table edge — where things land and pile up at the end of the day. Organization is the last thing on your mind when you walk through the door. You decompress first. The pile comes later.

I framed the whole project around that window: The First 5 Seconds. Coming home isn't a moment for organization. It's a moment of release. Stuff accumulates on chairs and counters because nothing was designed to accept that behavior. Every entry product on the market assumes you want to organize. It doesn't.

The spot by the door — keys, wallet, mail, sunglasses, AirPods. Every entry, same pile.

Pattern 01

Drop

Keys, bags, and daily items are set down immediately — without intention, without a destination in mind.

Pattern 02

Decompress

After a long day, users prioritize relief over organization. The coat comes off first. Tidying comes later — if at all.

Pattern 03

Pile

Items accumulate in transitional spaces — tables, chairs, counters — because there's no intentional home for them near the door.

Stop designing against human behavior. Design with it.

Define

Design Brief

Every product I looked at assumed the user wanted to organize. The actual behavior — drop, decompress, pile — runs in the opposite direction. The brief was to stop fighting it.

The solution needed to feel like furniture, not storage hardware. It had to work across the full load range — bulky sports gear down low, small daily items up top — and let users reconfigure it without tools or drilling anything into a wall.

5 Must-Have Criteria — All Met
01

Drop points at all heights

Accommodates the full range of what people carry — from a basketball to a set of keys.

02

Residential aesthetic

Reads as furniture, not utility storage. Something that belongs in a home.

03

Freestanding

No wall mounting, no commitment, no hardware — entry spaces are hard to commit to.

04

Tool-free configurable

Reconfigure as your life changes — no tools, no instructions, no friction.

05

Variable load capacity

Every existing entry product handles one load type well. This one handles all of them — heavy gear at the bottom, small daily items at the top.

Competitive audit of coat racks, hall trees, wall-mount slatted systems, and console tables: no existing product passed all 5 criteria.

Develop

Form Exploration

The lean-against-the-wall geometry came from watching how things actually land near a door. People don't walk to a storage zone — they drop things within arm's reach. A tall form against the wall is already where things land.

The slatted panel system came from the configurability requirement. Vertical slats + horizontal rods that slide along the outer frame = every intersection is a potential hang point. That math works out to 112 distinct positions, none of them requiring tools.

Iteration — testing visual weight, load feel, and prop scale with headphones and bag

Refined angle — validating lean geometry and horizontal rod spacing

Hook detail — headphones seated in the slot, slot geometry in section

Concept Exploration

Four directions were explored against the five criteria. Three were cut.

Direction B, a wall-mounted version of the same slatted surface, had better lateral stability but required wall anchors — it excluded renters and the aesthetic collapsed into SKADIS territory. Direction D, a console with a suspended hook bar above, needed two anchor points and couldn't handle anything tall without clearance problems.

The hardest cut was Direction C — a modular stacking system with a slide-and-lock spine. Most mechanically interesting. But when the slide-lock was sketched at full scale it didn't resolve cleanly. More importantly: it made you configure the system before you could put anything on it. That's not how people actually arrive home. It had to accept whatever someone threw at it the second they walked in.

Direction A, the slatted panel with passing rods, was the only one that cleared all five. Warm wood, lean geometry, freestanding.

Adjustable shelf — positioned by hand, no tools

Detail — headphone hook, water bottle, coat hang, and full unit

Deliver

The System

The Landing Zone is a tall, freestanding slatted wood panel that leans against the entry wall. Horizontal rods thread through the slats and lock into place without tools. Hook, hang, or shelf anywhere across the surface. The wood reads as furniture — something that belongs in a living room, not a garage.

112 Points of Customization

Every slot is a potential hook, ledge, or rest. Lower positions handle bulky load — sports gear, bags, a basketball. Upper positions are for daily items that get grabbed on the way out: keys, headphones, a wallet. The system works however you actually use your entry, not the way you're supposed to.

  • Slatted wood panels — warm, residential aesthetic that reads as furniture, not utility storage
  • Adjustable horizontal rods — slide along the outer frame; reconfigure without tools
  • Variable load capacity — lower shelves handle bulky sports gear; upper positions handle small daily items
  • Freestanding lean design — no wall mounting, no commitment, no hardware in the wall

Once I stopped trying to force organization and started designing for the actual moment of arrival, the form resolved quickly.
